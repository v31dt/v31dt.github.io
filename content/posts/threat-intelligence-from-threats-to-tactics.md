On 7 October 2025, I attended the Howest Tech event **"Threat Intelligence: from Threats to Tactics"**. The event focused on preparing organisations for modern cyber threats and translating threat knowledge into practical decisions. The session was physical, ran from 19:00 to 21:00, and fit very closely with the way I want to grow as a cybersecurity student.

Threat intelligence is one of the areas I am genuinely interested in because it is directly applicable to the work I already enjoy: bug bounty, offensive security, malware analysis, and threat-centred thinking. A lot of beginner security work starts from a checklist: test this endpoint, try this payload, scan this surface. That has value, but it can become mechanical. Threat intelligence adds context. It asks who might attack, what they usually do, what infrastructure or techniques they rely on, and which signals matter before an incident becomes obvious.

That mindset is useful in bug bounty as well. Good bug bounty work is not only about finding a random vulnerability. It is about understanding what would matter to a real attacker and what would have actual impact for the organisation. Threat intelligence helps shape that thinking. It can guide recon, prioritise targets, and make reports stronger because the finding is connected to realistic attacker behaviour instead of being presented as an isolated technical issue.

The presentation also made me think about how defensive and offensive work overlap. Even when I approach security from the offensive side, I still need to understand how defenders classify threats, track tactics, and communicate risk. Frameworks and threat models are not just paperwork when they are used well. They give teams a shared language for discussing what is happening and why it matters.

I would recommend this event to cybersecurity students who want to move beyond tool usage and think more like analysts. For me, the main takeaway was that threat intelligence makes security work more grounded. It connects technical findings to attacker motivation, business risk, and practical response.
