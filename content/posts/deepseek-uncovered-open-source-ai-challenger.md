On 9 December 2025, I attended the Howest Tech&Meet **"DeepSeek Uncovered: The Open-Source AI Challenger from the East"** at Howest Campus Brugge Station. The session was given by Dimitri Casier, lecturer in Software Development and AI at Howest. It focused on DeepSeek, why it became such a visible name in the AI discussion, and how open-source AI models compare with more closed systems from companies like OpenAI and Anthropic.

I wanted to attend this talk because AI is no longer a side topic in cybersecurity or software development. It is already changing how people write code, search for vulnerabilities, analyse logs, produce reports, and learn technical subjects. DeepSeek is interesting because it sits in the middle of several debates at once: performance, transparency, cost, access, geopolitics, and trust.

The open-source angle stood out most to me. A model that is easier to inspect, run, or adapt gives students and developers more room to understand what is happening instead of only consuming an API from the outside. That matters for learning. It also matters for security, because black-box systems are difficult to evaluate properly. At the same time, openness does not automatically mean safety. If powerful models become easier to access, defenders and attackers both get better tools.

That is where the topic connects to my own interests. In offensive security and bug bounty, AI can speed up recon, payload generation, code review, and documentation. But it can also make people lazy if they stop checking the reasoning behind the output. The best use of AI, in my opinion, is not replacing technical thinking. It is using it to move faster while still staying critical.

The talk made me think more carefully about which AI tools I trust and why. Benchmarks are useful, but they do not tell the whole story. For real work, I also care about data handling, transparency, reliability, and whether the model helps me understand the problem better.

I would recommend this session to students because it treated AI as a real technical and social shift, not just as hype. DeepSeek was the main example, but the bigger question was how we should work with open AI systems responsibly.
