For my Professional Networking podcast assignment, I spoke with Pedro Paniago, Offensive Security Manager at PwC Belgium, bug hunter, and a distinct voice in Belgium's security scene.

## Topic

The episode focuses on the future of bug bounty, AI's influence on bug hunting, and how security researchers can build their own AI hackbot from scratch.

## Guest

Pedro Paniago is an Offensive Security Manager at PwC Belgium. Besides his professional work, he is active in bug hunting and regularly shares a practical view on offensive security, automation, and the changing role of AI in cybersecurity.

## Episode summary

We talked about AI's influence on bug hunting and cybersecurity in general. Mr. Paniago also shared tips on how to build an AI hackbot from scratch, together with advice on how to keep your edge in an AI-driven security field.

The conversation was useful because bug bounty is changing quickly. AI can help with recon, payload generation, triage, and repetitive analysis, but it does not replace judgement. The best researchers still need curiosity, technical depth, and the ability to verify what a tool suggests.

## Reflection

Preparing this episode pushed me to think about security communication differently. A good technical conversation is not only about asking advanced questions. It also means guiding the listener through the topic, giving the guest room to explain their thinking, and turning a complex subject into something useful for people who are still building their own path in cybersecurity.

The strongest takeaway for me was that AI is becoming part of the offensive security workflow, but it rewards people who already understand the fundamentals. A hackbot is only useful if the person building it knows what to ask, how to test the output, and when to ignore it.
