Hack The Future: AquaRadar

Posted on November 12, 2025

Hack The Future was a good reminder that a hackathon is not only about having a good idea. It is also about knowing what you already have, and not throwing that advantage away once the pressure starts.

Hackathon

  • Name: Hack The Future
  • Challenge: Gotta Catch ‘Em All
  • Project: AquaRadar
  • Focus: a Pokemon-like fish spotting game

Reflection

The challenge was called “Gotta Catch ‘Em All”. The idea was simple and fun: build something like a Pokemon-style game, but instead of catching fictional creatures, users would spot real fish. Our version became AquaRadar, a system where someone could upload an image, get a result, review it, and slowly build up a small memory of what had already been recognised.

I still like the concept. It had a clear user story, it was visual, and it had room to become better the more people used it. That last part mattered to me. A tool that learns from corrections feels more honest than one that pretends to know everything immediately. The user stays involved, and the system improves because of that interaction.

But the main thing I took from this hackathon was not technical. It was about leverage.

We made the mistake of starting too much from scratch. We had prepared work, earlier ideas, and parts of projects that could have given us a stronger base. Instead of building on that, we spent too much time trying to rebuild the foundation during the event itself. In a normal project, that might just be inefficient. In a hackathon, it can decide how far you get.

That is the part I regret most. Not the bugs, not the unfinished details, not even the technical choices. The real regret is that we let go of our advantage. If you already have something that works, even if it is rough, that is your starting position. You should protect it. Improve it, reshape it, adapt it to the challenge, but do not abandon it just because a clean restart feels more satisfying in the moment.

A hackathon rewards momentum. Every useful thing you bring with you is leverage: previous code, a tested idea, a working UI pattern, a small prototype, even just knowing what not to try again. Next time, I would spend less energy chasing a perfect fresh start and more energy extending what we already know how to build.
