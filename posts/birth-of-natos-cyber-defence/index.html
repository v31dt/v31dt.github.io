The Birth of NATO's Cyber Defence

Posted on December 2, 2025

On 2 December 2025, I attended the Howest Tech&Meet “The Birth of NATO’s Cyber Defence: Insights from an Insider” at Howest Campus Brugge Station. The speaker was Martin De Pauw, who has worked in military technology, networks, IT, and cyber within the NATO context. The talk focused on how NATO’s view of cyber changed over time, especially around 2016, when cyberspace was officially recognised as an operational domain next to land, sea, and air.

What I liked about this event was that it placed cybersecurity in a much bigger frame than the usual technical one. A lot of the security work I normally focus on is close to the machine: web applications, mobile apps, malware behaviour, vulnerability research, and offensive testing. This talk zoomed out. It showed cyber defence as a question of coordination, policy, trust, resilience, and international cooperation.

That matters because real cyber incidents do not stop at technical boundaries. An attack can start with one system, but the consequences can reach governments, companies, supply chains, and ordinary people. NATO’s perspective makes that clear. Cyber defence is not only about blocking malware or patching servers. It is about keeping critical systems working when the environment becomes hostile.

The part that stood out to me most was the idea that cyber has no clean borders. In a traditional military context, territory is easier to define. In cyberspace, infrastructure, identities, services, and attackers can be spread across countries in seconds. That makes attribution, response, and cooperation much harder. It also explains why cyber defence needs both technical specialists and people who understand strategy.

For me as a cybersecurity student, this event was useful because it connected offensive and defensive thinking to real-world impact. If I find a vulnerability, the technical details matter, but the broader question is what that weakness could mean for an organisation or society. This is the kind of perspective I want to keep building: not only how systems break, but why that matters.

I would recommend this session to students who want to understand cybersecurity beyond tools and labs. It showed that cyber defence is a shared responsibility, and that technical knowledge becomes much stronger when it is connected to context.
